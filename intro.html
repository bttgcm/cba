1  Introduction

1.1 Background

When looking at the role that the Hindu-Kush-Himalaya (HKH) has in terms of water supply the focus is often given to the population living downhill covering 47% of world population (Pomeranz et al., n.d. ) with most attention given to glaciers and snowpacks. Differently from this approach, this research will focus on the 13 million people living on the hills and mountains of the HKH and on the water source that over 80% of these local communities use, namely, springs Poudel and Duex (2017). Water springs are not only used for drinking water but for a variety of additional uses such as livestock feeding, cleaning, irrigation. Aside from practical use they also carry symbolic and cultural values.

These particular conditions related to spring water, have some important implication in the way they are studied: the focus will need to shift from the watershed to the spring-shed, from glaciers to precipitation and recharge possibilities within the spring-shed. The characteristics of this situation (relatively small population affected, a different conceptual framework, lack of data) have probably caused the existing gap in research (Negi and Joshi 2002; Merz et al. 2003; Tambe et al. 2012).

1.2 Problem statement

Historically, springs have been managed at community level with different model of governance, the current challenges caused by climate change, different land use, variation of consumption pattern, are placing in a state of crisis the existing governance systems. Remediation and revival activity come at a cost and this is condition is confronted with the general perception of water as a free good by local communities in Nepal (Merz et al. 2003).

The identification of revival / remedial practices is therefore challenged by a lack of resources, the complexity of the problem and the necessary mobilization of stakeholders. Within this problematic, cost benefit analysis (CBA) can support decision makers in identifying the most cost-effective solutions, estimating benefit for the community and potentially support a case for intervention. This study aims at carrying a CBA for two springs that have been addressed by revival practices in Namobuddha Municipality, Nepal.

1.3 Research objectives

  1. Map the costs and benefits from implementing spring revival activities in the two selected sites

  2. Quantify and compare the benefits versus the costs of implementing spring revival activities in the two selected sites

  3. Estimate the impact of different factors in the cost-benefit analysis (via sensitivity analysis)

  4. Communicate the finding in an understandable way for the stakeholders and decision makers
